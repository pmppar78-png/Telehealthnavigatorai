Panic Attacks & Online Support

Educational information – not medical advice or diagnosis

Panic attacks are intense, frightening experiences that can make you feel like you're dying or losing control. The good news: panic is highly treatable, and online therapy provides effective, accessible support. This guide explains what panic attacks are, how treatment works, and where to find help.

What Is a Panic Attack?

A panic attack is a sudden surge of intense fear or discomfort that peaks within minutes. Common symptoms include:

  • Physical symptoms: Racing or pounding heart, sweating, trembling, shortness of breath, chest pain, nausea, dizziness, chills or hot flashes, numbness or tingling
  • Cognitive symptoms: Fear of losing control, fear of dying, feeling detached from yourself (derealization/depersonalization)

Panic attacks typically last 5-20 minutes but can feel much longer. They're terrifying but not physically dangerous—your body is activating its fight-or-flight response inappropriately.

Panic Attacks vs. Panic Disorder

Many people experience one or two panic attacks in their lifetime. Panic disorder is diagnosed when:

  • Recurrent, unexpected panic attacks
  • Persistent worry about having more attacks (at least one month)
  • Significant changes in behavior to avoid attacks (e.g., avoiding certain places)
  • Symptoms aren't caused by substances or another medical condition

Only a licensed mental health professional can diagnose panic disorder.

Why Seek Help?

Untreated panic can lead to:

  • Agoraphobia: Avoiding places where escape might be difficult during a panic attack
  • Social isolation: Withdrawing from activities and relationships
  • Anticipatory anxiety: Constant fear of the next attack
  • Depression: Often develops alongside chronic panic
  • Reduced quality of life: Panic dictating where you go and what you do

The great news: panic disorder responds very well to treatment. Most people see significant improvement.

Evidence-Based Treatments for Panic

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

The gold-standard treatment for panic disorder. CBT helps you:

  • Understand the panic cycle and how thoughts fuel symptoms
  • Challenge catastrophic interpretations of bodily sensations
  • Learn that panic sensations, while uncomfortable, aren't dangerous
  • Gradually face avoided situations (exposure therapy)
  • Develop coping skills for managing symptoms

Interoceptive Exposure

A specific technique where you deliberately induce panic-like sensations (spinning, hyperventilating) in a controlled way to reduce fear of the sensations themselves.

Medication

Several medication types can help:

  • SSRIs/SNRIs: First-line medications that reduce panic frequency over time
  • Benzodiazepines: Fast-acting but typically short-term due to dependency risk
  • Beta-blockers: Can reduce physical symptoms like rapid heartbeat

Many people benefit most from combining medication with therapy.

Online Therapy for Panic Attacks

Telehealth is particularly well-suited for panic treatment:

  • Access from safety: Work with a therapist from home if leaving triggers panic
  • Consistent treatment: Don't miss sessions due to avoidance
  • Gradual exposure support: Your therapist can guide virtual exposure exercises
  • Between-session support: Messaging for reassurance and skill reinforcement

Coping During a Panic Attack

These techniques can help when panic strikes:

  • Remind yourself: "This is a panic attack. It's uncomfortable but not dangerous. It will pass."
  • Slow your breathing: Breathe in for 4 counts, hold for 4, out for 6-8. Focus on extending the exhale.
  • Ground yourself: Use the 5-4-3-2-1 technique—name 5 things you see, 4 you hear, 3 you can touch, 2 you smell, 1 you taste.
  • Stay present: Focus on concrete details around you rather than "what if" thoughts.
  • Don't fight it: Resisting panic often intensifies it. Ride the wave and let it pass.
  • Move gently: Walking slowly or gentle stretching can help discharge adrenaline.

When to Seek Urgent Care

Panic attack symptoms can mimic serious medical conditions. Seek emergency care if:

  • This is your first panic-like episode (rule out medical causes)
  • You have chest pain and aren't sure if it's panic or cardiac
  • Symptoms don't resolve within 30 minutes
  • You have risk factors for heart disease
  • You're having thoughts of harming yourself

Crisis Resources: If you're in emotional crisis, contact the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (call or text 988). For medical emergencies, call 911.
