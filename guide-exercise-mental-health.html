Exercise & Mental Health

Published January 2026 · Educational information – not medical advice or diagnosis

The evidence is clear: physical activity is one of the most powerful tools for mental health. Exercise can reduce symptoms of depression and anxiety, improve mood, boost energy, and enhance overall wellbeing. This guide explores the science behind exercise and mental health and offers practical strategies for making movement part of your wellness routine.

How Exercise Affects the Brain

Physical activity influences mental health through multiple mechanisms:

  • Neurotransmitters: Exercise increases serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine—brain chemicals that regulate mood
  • Endorphins: Natural painkillers that produce feelings of wellbeing
  • BDNF: Brain-derived neurotrophic factor promotes new brain cell growth and connections
  • Stress response: Regular exercise reduces cortisol and trains the body to handle stress better
  • Inflammation: Exercise reduces inflammation, which is linked to depression
  • Sleep: Physical activity improves sleep quality, which benefits mood
  • Self-efficacy: Accomplishing exercise goals builds confidence and sense of control

Exercise and Depression

Research consistently shows exercise is effective for depression:

  • Exercise can be as effective as antidepressants for mild to moderate depression
  • Combining exercise with therapy or medication often produces better outcomes than either alone
  • Both aerobic exercise (cardio) and resistance training (weights) show benefits
  • Effects are seen with as little as 30 minutes, three times per week
  • Benefits appear within weeks and increase with continued practice

Challenge: Depression often makes starting exercise feel impossible. Start extremely small and consider professional support.

Exercise and Anxiety

Physical activity is highly effective for anxiety management:

  • Burns off stress hormones (adrenaline, cortisol)
  • Provides exposure to physical sensations similar to anxiety (racing heart, sweating), reducing fear of those sensations
  • Interrupts anxious thought patterns
  • Builds body confidence and sense of physical capability
  • Both high-intensity and gentle exercise (like yoga) show benefits

Exercise for Other Mental Health Concerns

  • ADHD: Improves focus, executive function, and helps manage hyperactivity
  • PTSD: Can help discharge trauma-related activation from the body
  • Sleep issues: Promotes deeper, more restorative sleep
  • Stress: One of the most effective stress management tools
  • Cognitive function: Improves memory, concentration, and mental clarity
  • Self-esteem: Builds body confidence and sense of accomplishment

How Much Exercise Do You Need?

Guidelines suggest:

  • Minimum: 150 minutes of moderate activity or 75 minutes of vigorous activity per week
  • Optimal: More is generally better, up to a point
  • Frequency: Benefits from spreading activity throughout the week

Key point: Some exercise is far better than none. If current guidelines feel overwhelming, start with whatever you can do—even 10 minutes has benefits.

Types of Exercise for Mental Health

Aerobic/Cardio

Walking, running, cycling, swimming, dancing—anything that raises heart rate.

  • Strongest evidence for depression and anxiety
  • Releases endorphins and burns off stress hormones
  • Can be done outdoors for added nature benefits

Strength Training

Weight lifting, resistance bands, bodyweight exercises.

  • Shown to reduce depression and anxiety
  • Builds physical confidence
  • Provides sense of progress and accomplishment

Mind-Body Exercise

Yoga, tai chi, Pilates, mindful movement.

  • Combines movement with mindfulness
  • Particularly helpful for anxiety and stress
  • Improves body awareness and relaxation

Nature-Based Exercise

Hiking, trail running, outdoor cycling, gardening.

  • Combines exercise with nature exposure
  • Nature alone has mental health benefits
  • Can feel less like "exercise" and more enjoyable

Starting When It Feels Impossible

When depression or anxiety makes movement feel overwhelming:

  • Start absurdly small: One minute. Ten steps. One stretch.
  • Pair with existing habit: "After my morning coffee, I'll stretch for 2 minutes"
  • Focus on showing up: Put on workout clothes—that's the win
  • Lower the bar: Any movement counts. Walking to the mailbox counts.
  • Make it enjoyable: Choose activities you actually like, or at least don't hate
  • Use music or podcasts: Make the time pass more pleasantly
  • Social element: Walk with a friend, join a class
  • Morning is often easier: Before the day depletes your motivation
  • Don't wait for motivation: Action often precedes motivation

Apps and Resources for Getting Moving

Fitness Apps

  • Nike Training Club: Free workouts for all levels
  • Down Dog: Customizable yoga, HIIT, and more
  • FitOn: Free video workouts
  • Couch to 5K: Gradual running program for beginners
  • Peloton: Classes for multiple activity types (subscription)

Mindful Movement

  • Calm - includes movement and stretching content
  • Headspace - mindful movement and yoga
  • Yoga with Adriene - free YouTube yoga for all levels
  • Insight Timer - guided movement meditations

Exercise as Part of Treatment

Exercise complements but doesn't replace professional treatment:

  • Tell your therapist: They can help integrate exercise into your treatment plan
  • Discuss with prescriber: Exercise can affect medication needs
  • Not a substitute: For moderate to severe symptoms, professional treatment is essential
  • Be patient: Benefits accumulate over weeks; give it time

Telehealth Support

  • BetterHelp - therapists can help address barriers to exercise
  • Talkspace - integrate exercise into behavioral change
  • Headway - find therapists who incorporate lifestyle factors

Making It Sustainable

  • Find what you enjoy: The best exercise is the one you'll actually do
  • Build slowly: Gradually increase duration and intensity
  • Be consistent, not intense: Regular moderate exercise beats occasional intense sessions
  • Don't rely on motivation: Create habits, systems, and environmental cues
  • Forgive setbacks: Missed days happen; just restart
  • Track for awareness: But don't let tracking become pressure
  • Celebrate small wins: Every movement session is an accomplishment
