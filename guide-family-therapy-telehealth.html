Family Therapy via Telehealth

Educational information – not medical advice or diagnosis

Families face unique challenges that affect everyone in the household. Online family therapy has made it possible to bring family members together for healing, even when schedules are complicated or members live in different locations. This guide explores virtual options for family counseling and how telehealth can support your family's wellbeing.

When to Consider Family Therapy

Family therapy can help with a wide range of issues:

  • Communication breakdowns: Family members struggle to talk or listen to each other
  • Parent-child conflict: Recurring battles over rules, boundaries, or expectations
  • Sibling rivalry: Conflict between children that parents can't resolve
  • Blended family challenges: Adjusting to stepparents, stepsiblings, or new family structures
  • Divorce or separation: Helping children cope with family changes
  • Mental health issues: Supporting a family member with depression, anxiety, or other conditions
  • Substance use: When addiction affects family dynamics
  • Grief and loss: Processing death or other losses together
  • Life transitions: Moving, job changes, children leaving home
  • Behavioral concerns: Addressing a child's challenging behaviors

Benefits of Online Family Therapy

Virtual family sessions offer distinct advantages:

  • Scheduling ease: Coordinate one appointment instead of multiple schedules for travel
  • Geographic flexibility: Include family members who live far away
  • Natural environment: Sessions from home can reveal real family dynamics
  • Reduced resistance: Some family members are more willing to try online therapy
  • Children's comfort: Kids may feel more at ease in familiar surroundings
  • Continuity: Maintain sessions during family travel or changes
  • Privacy: No need for the whole family to be seen entering a therapist's office

Family Therapy Approaches

Structural Family Therapy

Examines family structure, hierarchies, and boundaries to identify and change problematic patterns.

Strategic Family Therapy

Focuses on specific problems and developing strategies to address them, often with homework assignments.

Narrative Family Therapy

Helps families rewrite the stories they tell about themselves and their problems.

Bowenian Family Therapy

Focuses on multigenerational patterns and helping individuals differentiate while staying connected.

Functional Family Therapy

Evidence-based approach particularly effective for adolescent behavioral problems.

Parent-Child Interaction Therapy (PCIT)

For young children with behavioral issues, coaching parents in real-time during interactions.

Online Family Therapy Options

Therapy Platforms

  • BetterHelp - licensed marriage and family therapists for family sessions
  • Talkspace - family therapy options with licensed professionals
  • Headway - find in-network family therapists
  • Grow Therapy - insurance-covered family counseling
  • Rula - family therapy covered by many insurance plans

Teen and Adolescent Services

  • TeenCounseling (by BetterHelp) - for teens 13-19, with optional family sessions
  • Joon Care - mental health support for teens and young adults
  • Charlie Health - intensive outpatient for teens with family component

Parenting Support

  • Triple P (Positive Parenting Program) - evidence-based parenting support
  • Parent-Child Interaction Therapy - available via telehealth from trained providers
  • Little Otter - family mental health for children and parents

Support Resources

  • Calm - family-friendly mindfulness content
  • Headspace - meditation for the whole family
  • Mightier - emotional regulation games for children

What to Expect in Online Family Therapy

  1. Initial assessment: Understanding each family member's perspective and the family system
  2. Goal setting: Identifying what the family wants to achieve together
  3. Session format: May include whole-family sessions, parent-only sessions, or individual check-ins
  4. Skill building: Learning new communication and problem-solving techniques
  5. Practice at home: Implementing changes between sessions
  6. Progress review: Regular assessment of family functioning

Practical Considerations for Online Family Sessions

  • Technology setup: Ensure everyone can see and hear clearly (consider a larger screen)
  • Participation: Discuss with the therapist who should attend which sessions
  • Private space: Find a room where you won't be interrupted
  • Children's engagement: Therapists use age-appropriate techniques to include younger family members
  • Session length: Family sessions are often longer than individual therapy (60-90 minutes)
  • Remote family members: Those in different locations can join from their own devices

When Online Family Therapy May Not Be Enough

Consider in-person or more intensive options when:

  • There are safety concerns including abuse or violence
  • A family member is in acute crisis
  • Young children need hands-on therapeutic techniques
  • Technology barriers prevent effective participation
  • The family needs a more intensive level of care

If there is abuse or violence: Contact the Childhelp National Child Abuse Hotline at 1-800-422-4453 or the National Domestic Violence Hotline at 1-800-799-7233.

Supporting Family Therapy Between Sessions

  • Practice new communication techniques as a family
  • Complete any homework or exercises assigned by your therapist
  • Schedule regular family meetings to discuss concerns
  • Model healthy emotional expression
  • Acknowledge and celebrate progress
  • Be patient—family change takes time

Costs and Insurance

Family therapy costs vary:

  • Sessions typically cost more than individual therapy due to length and complexity
  • Many insurance plans cover family therapy when medically necessary
  • Some platforms offer family therapy at set rates
  • Check if your EAP includes family counseling sessions
  • Some therapists offer sliding scale fees for families
