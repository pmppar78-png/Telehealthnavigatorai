Borderline Personality Disorder & Online Therapy

Published January 2026 · Educational information – not medical advice or diagnosis

Borderline personality disorder (BPD) involves patterns of emotional intensity, relationship difficulties, and self-image challenges. While BPD can feel overwhelming, effective treatments exist, and many people experience significant improvement. Telehealth has expanded access to specialized BPD treatment, including Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT). This guide provides educational information about BPD and online care options.

Understanding Borderline Personality Disorder

BPD is a mental health condition characterized by pervasive patterns of instability in emotions, self-image, and relationships, along with impulsive behaviors. It affects approximately 1.4% of the adult population and is more commonly diagnosed in women, though it occurs in all genders.

Common Experiences

  • Emotional intensity: Feeling emotions very deeply and having difficulty regulating emotional responses
  • Fear of abandonment: Intense worry about being rejected or left by people you care about
  • Unstable relationships: Patterns of idealizing then devaluing others, or relationships that alternate between closeness and conflict
  • Identity disturbance: Unstable sense of self, values, or goals
  • Impulsivity: Acting on urges in ways that may be harmful (spending, substances, risky behaviors)
  • Self-harm: Engaging in self-injurious behaviors or having recurrent suicidal thoughts
  • Emptiness: Chronic feelings of emptiness or boredom
  • Anger: Difficulty controlling anger or having intense, inappropriate anger
  • Dissociation: Feeling disconnected from oneself or reality during stress

Only licensed mental health professionals can diagnose BPD through comprehensive evaluation. Many people have some of these experiences without having BPD.

Causes and Context

BPD develops from a combination of factors:

  • Biological factors: Brain differences in emotion regulation and impulse control
  • Environmental factors: Childhood experiences including trauma, neglect, or invalidating environments
  • Genetic factors: Family history may increase risk

BPD is not a choice or character flaw. It's a real condition that develops for understandable reasons and responds to treatment.

Evidence-Based Treatments

Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT)

DBT is the most researched and effective treatment for BPD. It typically includes:

  • Individual therapy: Weekly sessions focusing on current challenges
  • Skills training group: Weekly classes teaching four skill modules
  • Phone coaching: Brief calls for skill application in crisis moments
  • Therapist consultation team: Support for providers

DBT Skill Modules

  • Mindfulness: Being present and aware without judgment
  • Distress tolerance: Surviving crisis moments without making things worse
  • Emotion regulation: Understanding and managing intense emotions
  • Interpersonal effectiveness: Navigating relationships while maintaining self-respect

Other Effective Treatments

  • Mentalization-Based Therapy (MBT): Understanding your own and others' mental states
  • Schema-Focused Therapy: Addressing deep-seated patterns from early life
  • Transference-Focused Psychotherapy: Working through relationship patterns in therapy
  • General Psychiatric Management: Practical, case-management approach

Medication

While no medication is specifically approved for BPD, medications may help manage specific symptoms like depression, anxiety, or mood instability. Medication is typically most helpful when combined with therapy.

Online Therapy for BPD

Telehealth has made BPD treatment more accessible:

Benefits of Online BPD Treatment

  • Access to specialists: DBT-trained therapists may be limited in some areas
  • Consistent attendance: Easier to maintain treatment engagement
  • Skills groups: Virtual DBT groups can be effective
  • Comfort: Some find it easier to engage from home
  • Flexibility: Accommodate work and life schedules

Online Therapy Platforms

  • BetterHelp - search for DBT-trained therapists
  • Talkspace - licensed therapists with DBT experience
  • Headway - find in-network DBT providers
  • Grow Therapy - insurance-covered therapy options
  • Rula - therapist matching service

Specialized DBT Resources

  • DBT-Linehan Board Certification: Directory of certified DBT clinicians at dbt-lbc.org
  • Behavioral Tech: Training organization founded by Marsha Linehan
  • DBT Self-Help: Online resources and skill information

Online Psychiatry

  • Cerebral - psychiatric care and therapy
  • Talkiatry - in-network psychiatry
  • Brightside - therapy and medication management

What to Look for in a Therapist

When seeking BPD treatment:

  • Look for training in evidence-based BPD treatments, especially DBT
  • Ask about their experience treating BPD specifically
  • Inquire whether they offer comprehensive DBT (individual therapy plus skills group) or DBT-informed therapy
  • Discuss how they handle crisis situations
  • Ensure they have a non-judgmental, validating approach

Self-Help and Support Resources

  • DBT Skills Training Handouts and Worksheets by Marsha Linehan - comprehensive skill resources
  • Woebot - AI support with DBT-based skills
  • r/BPD and r/dbtselfhelp - Reddit communities for peer support
  • Emotions Matter - nonprofit supporting people with BPD
  • NEABPD (National Education Alliance for BPD) - education and family resources

When More Intensive Care Is Needed

Consider higher levels of care if:

  • Outpatient therapy isn't providing enough support
  • You're experiencing frequent crises
  • Self-harm or suicidal behaviors are escalating
  • You need more structure and support to stay safe

Options may include intensive outpatient programs (IOP), partial hospitalization programs (PHP), or residential treatment with specialized BPD tracks.

Crisis Resources: If you're in crisis or having thoughts of self-harm, contact the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (call or text 988), use the Crisis Text Line (text HOME to 741741), or go to your nearest emergency room.

Recovery and Hope

Many people with BPD experience significant improvement with treatment:

  • Research shows most people no longer meet diagnostic criteria for BPD after several years of treatment
  • Skills learned in DBT become more automatic over time
  • Relationships can improve as communication and regulation skills develop
  • The intensity of symptoms typically decreases with age and treatment
  • Recovery is possible, even if progress feels slow at times

For Loved Ones

If someone you care about has BPD:

  • Educate yourself about the condition
  • Consider family therapy or a DBT family skills group
  • Practice validation while maintaining boundaries
  • Take care of your own mental health
  • Connect with support groups like those offered by NEABPD
  • Remember that recovery is possible
