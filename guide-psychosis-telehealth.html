Psychosis & Telehealth Support

Educational information – not medical advice or diagnosis

Psychosis refers to experiences where someone loses touch with reality, often involving hallucinations or delusions. It can be frightening for both the person experiencing it and their loved ones. Understanding psychosis and knowing where to find help is an important first step. This guide provides educational information about psychosis and how telehealth may support care.

Understanding Psychosis

Psychosis is not a condition itself, but rather a collection of symptoms that can occur in various mental health conditions. During a psychotic episode, a person's thoughts and perceptions are disrupted, making it difficult to distinguish what is real from what is not.

Common Symptoms

  • Hallucinations: Sensing things that aren't there—most commonly hearing voices, but can also include seeing, feeling, smelling, or tasting things others don't perceive
  • Delusions: Strongly held beliefs that aren't based in reality—such as believing one is being watched, has special powers, or is receiving hidden messages
  • Disorganized thinking: Difficulty organizing thoughts, speaking in ways that are hard to follow, jumping between unrelated topics
  • Altered behavior: Acting in unusual ways, difficulty with daily activities, social withdrawal

Conditions That May Include Psychosis

  • Schizophrenia and schizoaffective disorder
  • Bipolar disorder with psychotic features
  • Major depression with psychotic features
  • Brief psychotic disorder
  • Substance-induced psychosis
  • Medical conditions affecting the brain

Only qualified healthcare professionals can determine the cause of psychotic symptoms through comprehensive evaluation.

The Importance of Early Intervention

Early intervention in psychosis leads to significantly better outcomes. The period before and during a first episode of psychosis, often called the "prodrome," may include:

  • Difficulty thinking clearly or concentrating
  • Suspiciousness or uneasiness around others
  • Decline in self-care or personal hygiene
  • Spending more time alone than usual
  • Strong emotions or no emotions at all
  • Unusual or exaggerated beliefs
  • Hearing, seeing, or sensing things others don't

If you notice these changes in yourself or someone you care about, reaching out to a mental health professional can make a meaningful difference.

Treatment Approaches

Psychosis is treatable. Many people who experience psychosis recover well with proper care:

Coordinated Specialty Care (CSC)

The gold standard for first-episode psychosis is coordinated specialty care, which includes:

  • Medication management
  • Individual and/or family therapy
  • Supported education and employment
  • Case management
  • Peer support

Medication

Antipsychotic medications help manage symptoms and reduce the risk of future episodes. Finding the right medication and dose often takes time and careful monitoring with a psychiatrist.

Therapy

  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Psychosis (CBTp): Helps develop coping strategies for symptoms
  • Family therapy: Supports family understanding and communication
  • Social skills training: Builds confidence in social situations

Role of Telehealth

While active psychosis typically requires in-person care, telehealth can support treatment in several ways:

Appropriate Uses of Telehealth

  • Follow-up appointments: Once stable, routine check-ins with psychiatrists and therapists
  • Medication monitoring: Regular reviews of medication effectiveness and side effects
  • Family sessions: Include family members in treatment regardless of location
  • Early intervention: Quick access to providers when warning signs emerge
  • Care coordination: Communication between treatment team members
  • Education: Learning about the condition and recovery

When In-Person Care Is Needed

  • During active psychotic episodes
  • Initial evaluation and diagnosis
  • When safety is a concern
  • Significant medication changes
  • When symptoms interfere with virtual communication

Resources and Support

Early Psychosis Programs

Many areas have specialized programs for early psychosis, often called Coordinated Specialty Care programs. The SAMHSA Early Serious Mental Illness Treatment Locator can help find programs: findtreatment.gov

Online Psychiatry Services

  • Talkiatry - in-network psychiatry with experienced providers
  • Cerebral - psychiatric care and therapy
  • Minded - medication management

Note: Not all telehealth platforms accept patients with psychotic disorders. Always verify provider experience with your specific concerns.

Support Organizations

  • NAMI (National Alliance on Mental Illness): Education, support groups, and resources
  • Schizophrenia and Psychosis Action Alliance: Advocacy and community support
  • Hearing Voices Network: Peer support for voice hearers
  • EASA (Early Assessment and Support Alliance): Early psychosis resources

If You're Experiencing Symptoms

If you're noticing changes in your thinking or perception that concern you:

  • Talk to someone you trust about what you're experiencing
  • Reach out to a mental health professional or your doctor
  • Remember that experiencing these symptoms doesn't mean you're "going crazy"—many people experience psychosis and recover well
  • Early help leads to better outcomes
  • Treatment works for many people

Crisis Resources: If you or someone you know is in crisis, contact the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (call or text 988), go to the nearest emergency room, or call 911.

For Family Members

If you're concerned about a loved one:

  • Express concern calmly and without judgment
  • Listen to their experiences, even if they seem unusual
  • Don't argue about whether their experiences are "real"—focus on how they're feeling
  • Encourage professional evaluation without being forceful
  • Learn about psychosis and treatment options
  • Connect with support groups like NAMI
  • Have a plan for what to do in a crisis
  • Take care of your own wellbeing
