Building a Relationship with Your Online Provider

Published January 2026 · Educational information – not medical advice

The therapeutic relationship—the connection between you and your provider—is one of the strongest predictors of positive outcomes in therapy. Building this relationship through a screen requires intentional effort but is absolutely possible. This guide explores how to develop a strong, trusting relationship with your online therapist or healthcare provider.

Why the Therapeutic Relationship Matters

Research consistently shows that the quality of the relationship between client and therapist is more important than the specific type of therapy used. A strong therapeutic alliance is characterized by:

  • Trust: Feeling safe to share vulnerable thoughts and feelings
  • Mutual respect: Being treated as a partner in your care
  • Empathy: Feeling genuinely understood
  • Agreement on goals: Working toward shared objectives
  • Collaboration: Being an active participant in treatment

These elements can develop just as strongly online as in person, with some adjustments.

Challenges of Building Connection Online

Virtual communication presents unique challenges:

  • Limited body language: Only seeing face and upper body
  • Eye contact differences: Looking at screen vs. camera creates different dynamics
  • Technical barriers: Audio delays, video quality issues
  • Physical distance: No shared physical space
  • Distractions: Competing demands at home
  • Emotional flatness: Screen can dampen emotional intensity

Recognizing these challenges is the first step to overcoming them.

Strategies for Building Connection

Create a Dedicated Space

Your environment signals to your brain that it's "therapy time":

  • Use the same private, quiet space for each session
  • Minimize distractions and interruptions
  • Have comfort items nearby (water, tissues, blanket)
  • Create ritual—make tea, take a breath before starting

Optimize Your Technology

Technical quality affects connection:

  • Position camera at eye level for natural "eye contact"
  • Use good lighting so your face is clearly visible
  • Test audio before sessions—being heard clearly matters
  • Use headphones for better audio and privacy
  • Close other applications to be fully present

Be Intentionally Present

Engagement requires extra effort online:

  • Silence your phone and close other tabs
  • Look at the camera when speaking (creates eye contact for them)
  • Nod and respond verbally more than you might in person
  • Name distractions if they occur rather than pretending they didn't
  • Show up on time and treat it as seriously as an in-person appointment

Communicate Openly

Clear communication is essential online:

  • Tell your therapist if you're struggling to connect virtually
  • Speak up if you can't hear or see them well
  • Ask for clarification if something isn't clear
  • Share feedback about what's working and what isn't
  • Be more explicit about your emotional state—"I'm feeling anxious right now"

Building Trust Over Time

Be Patient

Trust develops gradually:

  • Give the relationship at least 3-4 sessions before judging
  • It's normal to feel awkward or guarded at first
  • Vulnerability takes time—you don't need to share everything immediately
  • Notice small moments of connection and build on them

Take Appropriate Risks

Trust grows through vulnerability:

  • Share something slightly beyond your comfort zone
  • Express disagreement or discomfort with your therapist
  • Bring up difficult topics even when it's hard
  • Notice how your therapist responds to your vulnerability

Notice Safety Signals

Signs your provider is trustworthy:

  • They listen without interrupting
  • They remember details from previous sessions
  • They respond with empathy, not judgment
  • They acknowledge their own limitations
  • They respect your boundaries
  • They explain their approach and reasoning

When Connection Isn't Happening

Signs the Fit May Be Off

  • You consistently feel misunderstood
  • You dread or frequently cancel sessions
  • You hold back important information
  • You feel judged or dismissed
  • You're not making progress over time
  • Your values or approach don't align

What to Do

  1. Discuss it directly: Tell your therapist you're struggling to connect
  2. Give feedback: Explain specifically what isn't working
  3. Consider adjustments: Maybe a different session time or format would help
  4. Try a few more sessions: With direct communication, things may improve
  5. Consider switching: If nothing changes, it's okay to try a different provider

Switching Therapists

Platforms like BetterHelp and Talkspace make switching easy. Finding the right fit is important—don't settle for a relationship that isn't working. Use services like Headway or Rula to explore more options.

Leveraging Online-Specific Advantages

Online therapy offers unique opportunities for connection:

Between-Session Communication

  • Many platforms offer messaging between sessions
  • Share thoughts as they come up, not just in weekly sessions
  • Send updates about homework or progress
  • Reach out during difficult moments (within platform guidelines)

Home Environment

  • You may feel more relaxed in your own space
  • Your therapist can see glimpses of your real environment
  • Pets or comfort objects can be present
  • Less formality can support openness

Flexibility

  • Easier to maintain consistent attendance
  • Can attend from different locations
  • More scheduling options
  • No commute stress before sessions

Questions to Ask Your Provider

To strengthen your relationship, consider asking:

  • "What approach do you typically use, and why?"
  • "How do you handle it when clients disagree with you?"
  • "How will we know if therapy is working?"
  • "What's the best way to communicate with you between sessions?"
  • "How do you handle situations when a client is struggling virtually?"
  • "What should I do if I'm having a hard week?"

Special Considerations

For Therapy Beginners

If this is your first therapy experience:

  • It's normal not to know what to expect
  • Ask your therapist to explain anything that's unclear
  • There's no "wrong" way to do therapy
  • Your therapist wants to help you feel comfortable

For Those with Past Negative Therapy Experiences

If previous therapy didn't go well:

  • Share your past experiences with your new therapist
  • Identify specifically what didn't work before
  • This provider is different—give them a fair chance
  • Your wariness is understandable and can be part of what you discuss

For Those Who Struggle with Vulnerability

  • Start with less threatening topics
  • The screen can actually help—there's some psychological distance
  • Use messaging to share things that are hard to say aloud
  • Go at your own pace—your therapist will meet you where you are
