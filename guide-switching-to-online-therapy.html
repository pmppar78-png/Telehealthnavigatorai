Switching from In-Person to Online Therapy

Educational information – not medical advice

Whether you're moving, seeking more convenience, or your current therapist now offers virtual sessions, transitioning from in-person to online therapy is increasingly common. This guide helps you navigate the switch while maintaining the progress you've made.

Reasons People Switch to Online Therapy

There are many valid reasons to transition from in-person to online therapy:

  • Geographic relocation: Moving to a new city or state
  • Convenience: Eliminating commute time and scheduling flexibility
  • Life changes: New work schedule, caregiving responsibilities, or mobility issues
  • Comfort: Preference for therapy from home environment
  • Cost: Online therapy can sometimes be more affordable
  • Access: Limited local therapists or long wait lists
  • Continuity: Wanting to maintain an existing therapeutic relationship

Staying with Your Current Therapist

When This Is Possible

You may be able to continue with your current therapist online if:

  • They offer telehealth services
  • They are licensed in the state where you will be located
  • Your insurance (if applicable) covers their telehealth services
  • They use a HIPAA-compliant platform

Steps to Transition

  1. Discuss the switch: Talk with your therapist about moving to virtual sessions
  2. Verify licensing: Confirm they can practice in your location
  3. Check insurance: Ensure coverage continues for telehealth
  4. Learn the platform: Get comfortable with whatever video system they use
  5. Set up your space: Create a private, comfortable environment for sessions

When Staying Isn't Possible

If your therapist isn't licensed in your new state or doesn't offer telehealth, you'll need to find a new provider. Ask your current therapist for:

  • Referrals to colleagues in your new area
  • A summary of your treatment and progress
  • Recommendations for online platforms
  • Guidance on finding therapists with similar approaches

Finding a New Online Therapist

Online Therapy Platforms

Several platforms can connect you with licensed therapists:

  • BetterHelp - Large network with diverse specialties
  • Talkspace - Offers messaging, video, and audio sessions
  • Online-Therapy.com - CBT-focused treatment
  • Calmerry - Affordable online therapy option

Insurance-Based Options

  • Headway - Find in-network therapists
  • Grow Therapy - Insurance-covered sessions
  • Rula - Fast matching with covered providers

What to Look For

When selecting a new online therapist, consider:

  • Experience with your specific concerns
  • Therapeutic approach similar to what has worked for you
  • Licensing in your current state
  • Availability that fits your schedule
  • Communication style you're comfortable with

What Changes in Online Therapy

The Physical Environment

Unlike an office visit, you control your therapy environment:

  • Location: You choose where to have sessions (home, car, private room)
  • Comfort items: Have pets, blankets, or other comforts nearby
  • Privacy management: You're responsible for ensuring a private space
  • Technical setup: Camera angle, lighting, and audio quality matter

Communication Dynamics

Online communication has some differences:

  • Body language is less visible (often just face and upper body)
  • Eye contact requires looking at the camera, not the screen
  • Slight audio/video delays may occur
  • Silence can feel different through a screen

Session Structure

Some aspects of sessions may change:

  • No waiting room or commute time
  • More flexibility in scheduling
  • Homework and resources may be shared digitally
  • Between-session communication might be easier (messaging options)

What Stays the Same

Many core elements of therapy remain unchanged:

  • Therapeutic relationship: The connection with your therapist is still central
  • Treatment approaches: CBT, DBT, psychodynamic therapy, etc. work online
  • Confidentiality: Privacy protections still apply
  • Professional standards: Licensed therapists follow the same ethics
  • Your goals: Treatment continues to focus on your objectives
  • Effectiveness: Research shows online therapy is as effective as in-person for many conditions

Maintaining Your Progress

Communicate Your History

If starting with a new therapist:

  • Request records transfer with proper authorization
  • Prepare a summary of your therapy journey
  • Share what approaches have worked (and haven't)
  • Discuss your goals and current progress
  • Mention any medications or other providers involved in your care

Stay Engaged

  • Complete homework and practice exercises
  • Continue journaling or mood tracking
  • Maintain healthy routines established in therapy
  • Use skills you've learned, even during the transition period

Allow Adjustment Time

It's normal to need time to adapt:

  • Give yourself 3-4 sessions to adjust to the new format
  • Communicate any discomfort or challenges to your therapist
  • Be patient with yourself and the process
  • Recognize that progress may temporarily slow during transition

Tips for a Successful Transition

Create a Dedicated Space

  • Choose a consistent, private location for sessions
  • Ensure good lighting and minimal background noise
  • Make the space comfortable and conducive to openness
  • Have headphones available for added privacy

Establish New Rituals

Create routines that signal "therapy time":

  • A few minutes of quiet reflection before sessions
  • Making a cup of tea or water before starting
  • Brief journaling after sessions to process
  • A short walk after difficult sessions

Embrace the Benefits

Online therapy offers unique advantages:

  • More therapist options to choose from
  • Easier to fit sessions into busy schedules
  • No travel time or expense
  • Ability to attend sessions when traveling
  • More comfortable environment for some topics

When In-Person May Still Be Needed

While online therapy works well for many, some situations may benefit from in-person care:

  • Certain trauma treatments requiring specific protocols
  • Severe mental health conditions needing intensive support
  • Group therapy that works better in person
  • When you don't have a private space for sessions
  • If you consistently struggle with technology

Discuss with your therapist if online format isn't meeting your needs.
