Online Support for Substance Use

Educational information – not medical advice or diagnosis

Substance use disorders are treatable conditions, and telehealth has made it easier than ever to access help. Whether you're concerned about alcohol, drugs, or other substances, online treatment can provide confidential, evidence-based support. This guide explores virtual options for substance use treatment and recovery.

Understanding Substance Use Disorders

Substance use exists on a spectrum, from casual use to substance use disorder (SUD). Signs that may indicate a problem include:

  • Using more than intended or for longer periods
  • Unsuccessful efforts to cut down or control use
  • Spending significant time obtaining, using, or recovering from substances
  • Cravings or strong urges to use
  • Continued use despite problems in relationships, work, or health
  • Tolerance (needing more to achieve the same effect)
  • Withdrawal symptoms when not using

Only licensed healthcare professionals can diagnose substance use disorders. Seeking help is a sign of strength, not weakness.

How Telehealth Supports Recovery

Online treatment offers several advantages for substance use support:

  • Privacy: Access treatment discreetly from home
  • Accessibility: Overcome transportation barriers and rural access issues
  • Continuity: Maintain treatment during travel or life changes
  • Flexibility: Fit treatment around work and family schedules
  • Immediate access: Connect with support when you need it most
  • Reduced stigma: Many find it easier to open up remotely

Evidence-Based Treatment Approaches

Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT)

FDA-approved medications combined with counseling and behavioral therapies. MAT is highly effective for:

  • Alcohol use disorder: Naltrexone, acamprosate, disulfiram
  • Opioid use disorder: Buprenorphine (Suboxone), naltrexone

Many of these medications can now be prescribed via telehealth.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

Helps identify triggers, develop coping strategies, and change patterns of thinking and behavior related to substance use.

Motivational Interviewing

A collaborative approach that helps strengthen personal motivation and commitment to change.

Contingency Management

Uses positive reinforcement to encourage abstinence and treatment participation.

12-Step Facilitation

Structured approach to engaging with support groups like AA or NA.

Levels of Care

Substance use treatment ranges from low to high intensity:

  • Outpatient: Weekly sessions, can be done entirely online
  • Intensive Outpatient (IOP): Multiple weekly sessions, often available virtually
  • Partial Hospitalization: Full-day programs, some virtual options
  • Residential/Inpatient: 24-hour care, requires in-person treatment
  • Medical Detox: Supervised withdrawal, requires in-person care

The appropriate level depends on severity, medical needs, and personal circumstances.

When Online Treatment May Not Be Enough

Seek in-person or higher-level care if:

  • Physical dependence requiring medical detox (especially alcohol, benzodiazepines, or opioids)
  • History of severe withdrawal symptoms or seizures
  • Active suicidal thoughts or self-harm
  • Unstable living situation or lack of safe environment
  • Co-occurring severe mental health conditions
  • Previous treatment failures requiring more intensive support

Warning: Alcohol and benzodiazepine withdrawal can be life-threatening. Never stop heavy use suddenly without medical supervision.

What to Expect in Online Treatment

  1. Assessment: Evaluation of substance use history, patterns, and medical needs
  2. Treatment planning: Personalized approach based on your goals and situation
  3. Medical consultation: If appropriate, evaluation for medication-assisted treatment
  4. Regular sessions: Individual therapy, group sessions, or both
  5. Support resources: Connection to recovery communities and tools
  6. Progress monitoring: Ongoing assessment and plan adjustment

Supporting Your Recovery

  • Be honest with your treatment providers about your use
  • Attend all scheduled sessions and follow your treatment plan
  • Build a support network of people who support your recovery
  • Identify and avoid triggers when possible
  • Develop healthy coping strategies for stress and cravings
  • Consider joining peer support groups
  • Address co-occurring mental health issues
  • Practice self-compassion—recovery is a process, not perfection
