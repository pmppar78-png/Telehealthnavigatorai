Online Therapy for Anxiety

Educational information – not medical advice or diagnosis

Anxiety is one of the most common mental health concerns, and online therapy has become an increasingly popular and effective way to get help. This guide explores telehealth options for anxiety, treatment approaches, and what to consider when seeking support.

Understanding Anxiety

Anxiety exists on a spectrum from normal, everyday nervousness to disorders that significantly impact daily life. Common types include:

  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD): Persistent, excessive worry about various aspects of life
  • Social Anxiety Disorder: Intense fear of social situations (see Social Anxiety Treatment Online)
  • Panic Disorder: Recurring panic attacks and fear of future attacks (see Panic Attacks & Online Support)
  • Specific Phobias: Intense fear of specific objects or situations
  • Health Anxiety: Excessive worry about having or developing serious illness

Only licensed healthcare professionals can diagnose anxiety disorders. This information is for educational purposes only.

How Online Therapy Can Help with Anxiety

Research has shown that online therapy can be as effective as in-person therapy for many people with anxiety. Benefits include:

  • Accessibility: Get help without leaving home—especially helpful if anxiety makes travel difficult
  • Comfort: Discuss sensitive topics from a familiar, safe environment
  • Consistency: Easier to maintain regular sessions
  • Flexibility: Work with therapists across a wider geographic area
  • Support between sessions: Many platforms offer messaging for ongoing support

Evidence-Based Treatments for Anxiety

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

The gold-standard treatment for anxiety, CBT helps you:

  • Identify anxious thought patterns
  • Challenge and reframe negative thinking
  • Develop coping strategies
  • Gradually face feared situations

Online-Therapy.com specializes in CBT-based treatment.

Exposure Therapy

Gradually facing anxiety-provoking situations in a controlled way to reduce fear response over time.

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)

Focuses on accepting difficult thoughts and feelings while taking action aligned with personal values.

Mindfulness-Based Approaches

Combining meditation and awareness techniques with therapy to manage anxiety. Apps like Calm and Headspace can complement therapy.

Medication

For some people, medication combined with therapy is most effective. Online psychiatry services like Cerebral, Brightside, and Talkiatry can evaluate and prescribe anxiety medications.

What to Expect in Online Anxiety Treatment

  1. Initial assessment: Your therapist will ask about your symptoms, history, and goals
  2. Treatment planning: Together you'll develop a plan tailored to your needs
  3. Regular sessions: Usually weekly, via video, phone, or messaging
  4. Homework: Often includes practicing techniques between sessions
  5. Progress monitoring: Regular check-ins on how you're doing
  6. Adjustment: Treatment may be modified based on your progress

When Online Therapy May Not Be Enough

Seek in-person or more intensive care if:

  • Anxiety is severely impacting daily functioning
  • You're experiencing panic attacks that feel uncontrollable
  • You have thoughts of harming yourself
  • You're unable to leave home due to anxiety (agoraphobia)
  • You have co-occurring substance use issues
  • You haven't improved with online treatment

Crisis Resources: If you're in crisis, contact the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (call or text 988) or go to your nearest emergency room.

Tips for Getting the Most from Online Anxiety Treatment

  • Be honest about your symptoms and experiences
  • Complete homework and practice exercises between sessions
  • Track your anxiety levels to monitor progress
  • Communicate openly with your therapist about what's working
  • Give treatment adequate time—improvement often takes weeks to months
  • Consider combining therapy with lifestyle changes (exercise, sleep, limiting caffeine)
