Dissociative Disorders & Telehealth

Published January 2026 · Educational information – not medical advice or diagnosis

Dissociative disorders involve disruptions in consciousness, memory, identity, emotion, perception, or sense of self. These experiences often develop as ways of coping with overwhelming stress or trauma. Understanding dissociation and finding appropriate care can be an important step toward healing. This guide provides educational information about dissociative disorders and how telehealth may support treatment.

Understanding Dissociation

Dissociation exists on a spectrum. Mild dissociation is common—like "zoning out" during a boring meeting or arriving somewhere without remembering the drive. However, when dissociation becomes frequent, severe, or distressing, it may indicate a dissociative disorder.

Common Dissociative Experiences

  • Depersonalization: Feeling detached from yourself, like you're observing yourself from outside your body, or feeling like you're in a dream
  • Derealization: Feeling that the world around you is unreal, foggy, or distant
  • Amnesia: Gaps in memory for personal information, events, or time periods that can't be explained by ordinary forgetting
  • Identity confusion: Uncertainty about who you are, your values, or your sense of self
  • Identity alteration: Shifts in identity, behavior, or sense of self that feel distinctly different

Types of Dissociative Disorders

Depersonalization-Derealization Disorder

Persistent or recurrent experiences of feeling detached from yourself (depersonalization) or your surroundings (derealization), while knowing that these feelings aren't actually real.

Dissociative Amnesia

Inability to recall important personal information, usually related to a traumatic or stressful event. This goes beyond ordinary forgetting and cannot be explained by another medical condition.

Dissociative Identity Disorder (DID)

Formerly called multiple personality disorder, DID involves the presence of two or more distinct identity states or personality states, along with gaps in memory for everyday events, personal information, or traumatic events.

Other Specified Dissociative Disorder (OSDD)

Dissociative symptoms that cause significant distress but don't fully meet criteria for other specific dissociative disorders.

Only qualified mental health professionals can diagnose dissociative disorders through comprehensive evaluation. Diagnosis can take time, as symptoms may overlap with other conditions.

Causes and Context

Dissociative disorders most commonly develop in response to overwhelming experiences, particularly:

  • Childhood trauma, abuse, or neglect
  • Severe stress or traumatic events
  • Situations where escape wasn't possible

Dissociation can be understood as the mind's way of protecting itself when experiences are too overwhelming to process. It's an adaptive response that becomes problematic when it persists beyond the original threat.

Treatment Approaches

Treatment for dissociative disorders typically involves specialized therapy focused on safety, stabilization, and processing traumatic material at a pace that feels manageable:

Phase-Oriented Treatment

  1. Phase 1 - Stabilization: Building safety, developing coping skills, and establishing a therapeutic relationship
  2. Phase 2 - Trauma processing: Working through traumatic memories when ready and stable
  3. Phase 3 - Integration and rehabilitation: Consolidating gains and building a fulfilling life

Therapeutic Approaches

  • Trauma-focused therapy: Processing traumatic experiences safely
  • EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing): Processing traumatic memories
  • Parts work / Internal Family Systems: Working with different parts of self
  • Grounding techniques: Staying present and managing dissociation
  • CBT for dissociation: Addressing unhelpful thoughts and behaviors

Medication

While no medication specifically treats dissociation, medications may help with co-occurring symptoms like depression, anxiety, or sleep problems.

Telehealth for Dissociative Disorders

Benefits of Online Treatment

  • Access to specialists: Dissociation-informed therapists can be rare in some areas
  • Comfort and safety: Attend sessions from a familiar, safe environment
  • Consistency: Maintain treatment during difficult periods
  • Flexibility: Schedule around needs and energy levels
  • Grounding support: Easier to use grounding objects and techniques at home

Considerations for Telehealth

  • Ensure a private, safe space for sessions
  • Have grounding tools nearby (ice, strong scents, textured objects)
  • Discuss with your therapist what to do if dissociation occurs during sessions
  • Have a plan for ending sessions if needed

Finding a Therapist

When seeking treatment for dissociative disorders:

  • Look for therapists with specific training in trauma and dissociation
  • Ask about their experience treating dissociative disorders
  • Inquire about their treatment approach and timeline
  • Ensure they understand that treatment takes time and should not be rushed

Online Therapy Platforms

  • BetterHelp - search for trauma-specialized therapists
  • Talkspace - licensed therapists with trauma experience
  • Headway - find in-network trauma specialists
  • Grow Therapy - insurance-covered care

Specialized Resources

  • ISSTD (International Society for the Study of Trauma and Dissociation): Find a therapist directory at isstd.org
  • Psychology Today: Filter for dissociative disorder specialization
  • EMDR International Association: Find EMDR-trained therapists

Grounding Techniques

Grounding helps bring you back to the present moment when dissociation occurs:

  • 5-4-3-2-1: Name 5 things you see, 4 you hear, 3 you can touch, 2 you smell, 1 you taste
  • Temperature: Hold ice cubes, splash cold water on your face, or step outside
  • Strong sensations: Smell something strong, eat something sour, or hold a rough texture
  • Movement: Stamp your feet, stretch, or squeeze a stress ball
  • Orientation: Say your name, the date, where you are, and that you are safe
  • Breathing: Slow, deep breaths focusing on the physical sensation

Self-Help and Support

  • Beauty After Bruises: Education and resources for trauma and dissociation
  • Discussing Dissociation: Blog with educational resources
  • PODS (Positive Outcomes for Dissociative Survivors): UK-based resources and training
  • Online support communities: Peer support through moderated forums

When to Seek Additional Support

Consider more intensive care if:

  • You're unable to maintain safety
  • Symptoms are significantly interfering with daily functioning
  • Outpatient treatment isn't providing enough support
  • You're experiencing crisis situations frequently

Crisis Resources: If you're in crisis or feeling unsafe, contact the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (call or text 988), the Crisis Text Line (text HOME to 741741), or go to your nearest emergency room.

Recovery and Hope

Recovery from dissociative disorders is possible. Treatment can help you:

  • Develop awareness and understanding of dissociative experiences
  • Build skills to stay present and grounded
  • Process traumatic experiences safely
  • Develop internal communication and cooperation (for DID/OSDD)
  • Build a more integrated sense of self
  • Create a fulfilling life beyond trauma

Recovery takes time and looks different for everyone. Progress isn't always linear, but healing is possible.
